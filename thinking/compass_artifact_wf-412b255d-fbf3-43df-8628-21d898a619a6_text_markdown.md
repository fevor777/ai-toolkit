# Critical Thinking Micro-Exercises in Apps, Courses, and Research: A Survey of Concrete Examples

This report catalogs concrete examples of short, game-like critical thinking exercises used in educational apps, MOOCs, academic studies, and journalism/media literacy projects. The focus is on the **actual format** of tasks — what the player or learner is asked to do moment to moment — and on **whether each task is genuinely "micro"** (a self-contained item that takes seconds to a few minutes) versus part of a longer lesson. Where available, published effectiveness data is summarized; where it isn't, the report flags items as anecdotal or unevaluated.

The exercises cluster into three skill families requested by the user: (1) fallacy detection and naming; (2) assumption-spotting / identifying hidden premises and manipulation techniques; and (3) distinguishing fact from opinion (or fact from inference). Some interventions cut across more than one category, and several of the most rigorously evaluated tools belong to the "inoculation theory" tradition led by Sander van der Linden's Cambridge Social Decision-Making Lab and John Cook's Cranky Uncle work.

## 1. Fallacy Detection: Naming and Explaining Logical Fallacies

### Cranky Uncle (John Cook, Monash University / George Mason)
Cranky Uncle is the clearest example of a true micro-exercise app dedicated to fallacy detection. It is free on iPhone, Android, and as a browser game, and was first launched in late 2020 with a 2.0 multilingual version a year later.

**Format.** A cartoon "cranky uncle" character introduces a manipulation technique from the FLICC taxonomy (Fake experts, Logical fallacies, Impossible expectations, Cherry picking, Conspiracy theories — the "logical fallacies" branch is broken into more than a dozen sub-fallacies including ad hominem, false dichotomy, slothful induction, jumping to conclusions, non sequitur, and more). After a brief explanation, players move to **quiz items that come in three formats**:
- **True/false items** — the player is shown a single statement (often containing a fallacy) and decides whether it is logically valid or fallacious. Some items are inherently true tautologies (e.g., "people are dying who never died before") to keep players honest.
- **"Pick the fallacy" items** — the player is shown four short statements and must pick which one contains a specific fallacy (e.g., "Which of these is cherry picking?").
- **"Name the fallacy" items** — a short cartoon or text claim is presented and the player picks the correct fallacy label from four options.

Each item takes a few seconds. Correct answers earn "cranky points," and as players accumulate points, the cartoon's mood escalates from "sullen" to "huffy" to "peevish." Cook explicitly designed the game to be played in micro-doses: players "by repeatedly practising the spotting of fallacies and denial techniques become faster and more proficient over time," moving toward expert heuristics.

**Skill trained.** Recognizing parallel-argument structure: the cartoons often deliver a fallacy in an absurd domain (e.g., "Birds fly, therefore gravity isn't real" → jumping to conclusions) so players learn the abstract reasoning error without being distracted by content. Cook's underlying research (Cook, Ellerton, & Kinkead 2018) found that 91% of climate-contrarian claims contained hidden premises with logical fallacies, which the game targets directly.

**Effectiveness.** Cook and colleagues' research on "logic-based inoculation" (Cook, Lewandowsky, & Ecker 2017, PLOS ONE) shows that exposing people to weakened-form fallacies neutralizes the influence of the corresponding misinformation. Classroom uses at Texas A&M (50–60-student climate course) had students play the game across two weeks, then write a 200–400-word denial argument using fallacies they learned. Quasi-experimental studies suggest improvements in fallacy recognition, though most published evaluations are pre/post within-subjects rather than randomized controlled trials. The game has been adopted in 17+ countries and adapted for vaccines, partnered with UNICEF and the Sabin Vaccine Institute for use in East Africa, Pakistan, and Ghana.

### "Think Again IV: How to Avoid Fallacies" (Duke University, Coursera)
Walter Sinnott-Armstrong and Ram Neta's MOOC is the most structured fallacy-naming course online. It is the fourth in a four-course "Introduction to Logic and Critical Thinking" specialization based on the older single course "Think Again: How to Reason and Argue."

**Format.** Each week has multiple short video segments (usually 5–15 minutes each) followed by **short ungraded comprehension quizzes** that ask students to identify whether a sample passage commits equivocation, ad hominem, appeal to authority, ambiguity, vagueness, circularity, or a similar named fallacy. A longer graded quiz comes at the end of each course.

**Skill trained.** Naming and explaining named fallacies (vagueness/ambiguity fallacies in one module, ad hominem and appeal-to-authority in another, fallacies of relevance and vacuity in another). Earlier courses in the series train assumption-spotting (see Section 2).

**Micro vs. lesson.** The video segments are full lessons; the inter-segment quizzes are the genuinely micro tasks. Reviewers on Class Central note the quizzes themselves are sometimes too easy and the lectures pace is slow, but the format does provide many bite-sized identification items.

**Effectiveness.** No randomized study of the Coursera version has been published. Older work on Sinnott-Armstrong's textbook approach and on critical-thinking instruction generally is captured by Abrami et al.'s meta-analyses (see Section 4).

### Argument Ninja Academy (Kevin deLaplante)
A philosopher-run subscription "dojo" framing critical thinking as a martial art with belts. The publicly available material is mostly podcast episodes and video lectures rather than micro-exercises. The program *plans* graded "sparring" exercises modeled on martial-arts drills, but there is no published effectiveness research and no large library of bite-sized interactive fallacy items as of mid-2026. This entry is included for completeness given the user's list, but it is closer to a curriculum than a micro-exercise app.

### TARA / LSAT-style critical reasoning items
Standardized critical-thinking admissions tests (TARA, LSAT logical reasoning, the older Cambridge Thinking Skills Assessment) all use a 60–90-second multiple-choice format where the test taker reads a short argument and chooses, for example, "Which of the following weakens the argument?" or "Which assumption does the argument depend on?" These are "test prep" exercises rather than gamified, but pedagogically they are arguably the oldest and best-validated micro-format for assumption-spotting and identifying fallacies like false cause, circular reasoning, and false dilemma.

## 2. Assumption-Spotting and Manipulation-Technique Recognition

This category is dominated by inoculation-theory games from Sander van der Linden's Cambridge Social Decision-Making Lab in collaboration with the Dutch agency DROG and Gusmanson Design.

### Bad News (Cambridge / DROG, 2018)
The original gamified inoculation intervention.

**Format.** The player runs a fake-news Twitter empire across six levels, each corresponding to one technique from the **DEPICT** taxonomy: **D**iscrediting opponents, **E**motional language use, **P**olarization, **I**mpersonation through fake accounts, **C**onspiracy theories, and **T**rolling. At each level the game presents a short scenario (e.g., a post breaking news about a politician) and asks the player to choose between two or three reply tweets, where the "right" choice is the one that successfully uses the manipulation technique without losing followers or credibility (the two on-screen meters). Players occasionally compose short tweets themselves. The whole game takes about 15 minutes; each individual decision is a 30–60-second item.

**Skill trained.** Recognizing manipulation techniques by producing them — "active inoculation" in McGuire's (1961) sense, with weakened doses delivered through the choice mechanic.

**Effectiveness.** This is the most rigorously evaluated game in this entire report.
- Roozenbeek & van der Linden (2019, *Palgrave Communications*) tested ~15,000 self-selected players in a pre/post within-design and found a 21% average reduction in perceived reliability of fake news headlines after one play.
- Basol, Roozenbeek & van der Linden (2020, *Journal of Cognition*) replicated the effect in a controlled design and found increased confidence in spotting misinformation, with both effects in the right direction (people only became more confident when they were also more accurate).
- Maertens et al. (2021) showed inoculation effects persisted at least one week — and up to three months when players received weekly assessment "boosters."
- A later replication on Indian true/false news headlines (PMC12705795) found **no improvement** in discrimination, suggesting the effect may be culture- or content-specific. This negative result deserves explicit mention.
- A 2025 signal-detection-theory meta-analysis by Simchon, Zipori, Teitelbaum, Lewandowsky & van der Linden (in *Current Opinion in Psychology*) re-examined the broader inoculation literature; results are mixed, with the games reliably increasing perceived manipulativeness but having more modest effects on actual discrimination accuracy.

### Harmony Square (Cambridge / DROG / US DHS-CISA / US State Department, 2020)
A 10-minute follow-up game focused on political disinformation and election interference.

**Format.** The player is hired as "Chief Disinformation Officer" of an idyllic neighborhood "mildly obsessed with democracy" and proceeds through four levels (Trolling, Emotion, Amplification, Escalation), each a sequence of short choice-based scenarios. Players bait the town's "living statue," spread falsehoods about a candidate for "bear controller," and set up a fake news site. Each scenario is a 30–60-second decision. Five techniques are taught: trolling, emotional-language exploitation, artificial amplification (bots / fake followers), conspiracy theories, and audience polarization.

**Effectiveness.** Roozenbeek & van der Linden (2020, *HKS Misinformation Review*) report a randomized controlled study finding that players found manipulative content significantly less reliable, were more confident in their judgments, and were less likely to share manipulative content. Effect translates roughly to 63% of players (vs. 37% of controls) finding misinformation significantly less reliable. Effects held across the political spectrum.

### Go Viral! (Cambridge / UK Cabinet Office / DROG, 2020)
The COVID-specific 5-minute browser game.

**Format.** Three short rounds covering fearmongering / emotional language, fake experts (fake doctors, fake remedies), and conspiracy theories (e.g., the 5G–COVID rumor). Each round is a click-through micro-scenario where the player chooses a manipulative tweet to post.

**Effectiveness.** Basol et al. (2021, *Big Data & Society*) ran two preregistered studies (n₁=1,771; n₂=1,777). 74% of Go Viral! players got better at sensing manipulation vs. 55% of Tetris controls — a 19-point gap. Effects on perceived manipulativeness and confidence persisted at least one week. UNESCO infographics produced smaller-but-significant effects.

### Jigsaw / YouTube Prebunking Videos (Roozenbeek, van der Linden, Lewandowsky & Goldberg, 2022)
Not a game but the largest field deployment of micro-inoculation. Reported in Roozenbeek et al. (2022, *Science Advances*).

**Format.** 90-second animated YouTube ads inoculating against five techniques: emotional language, incoherence, false dichotomies, scapegoating, and ad-hominem attacks. Within ~18 hours of viewing, users were prompted with a short BrandLift quiz containing one item with a manipulation technique embedded in a novel headline; they had to identify the technique.

**Effectiveness.** Across seven studies (~30,000 participants), the field experiment on YouTube showed treated viewers were 5–10% more likely than controls to correctly identify the technique they had been inoculated against. Click-through rates on the "learn more" ad were ~3× higher in the treatment group. Jigsaw subsequently rolled out the campaign in Poland, Slovakia, and the Czech Republic to address Ukrainian-refugee disinformation, and the 2024 EU-elections version reached 120 million users across 12 countries.

### "Think Again I: How to Understand Arguments" (Duke / Coursera)
The first course in the Sinnott-Armstrong / Neta sequence focuses heavily on **reconstructing arguments and identifying suppressed premises**.

**Format.** Sample passages (5–8 sentences) are presented, and short comprehension quizzes ask the student to (a) identify which sentence is the conclusion, (b) identify which sentence(s) are explicit premises, and (c) supply the missing or "suppressed" premise required to make the argument valid. These individual quiz items are bite-sized; the surrounding video lectures are not.

**Skill trained.** Argument decomposition into premise/conclusion structure, then explicit detection of unstated assumptions — the canonical philosophy-101 micro-task.

### Argument Mapping Tools: Rationale, MindMup, Kialo Edu
These tools provide a visual workbench for premise/conclusion analysis. They are not strictly micro-exercises, but each has been used to deliver short assumption-spotting tasks.

- **Rationale** (originally developed at Melbourne) presents a passage and asks the student to drag premise boxes into a tree, with the "Holding Hands" and "Rabbit Rule" heuristics enforcing that every inference step has all the premises needed (i.e., the student is forced to surface hidden premises shown in square brackets). Studies by Twardy, van Gelder, and colleagues report effect sizes around 0.7–0.8 standard deviations on standardized critical-thinking tests after a semester of use — about three times what a typical university semester yields, according to van Gelder (2015) and Davies (2012). These figures are the most-cited evidence for argument mapping; they come largely from one research group, so independent replication remains modest.
- **Kialo Edu** structures discussions as branching pro/con trees under a central thesis. Typical micro-tasks include "Reorder Claims" (students are given a deliberately disorganized argument tree and must move claims to logical positions and split combined claims) and "Mark Claim" (students annotate each claim with what's missing — a source, a sub-argument, etc.). These are short collaborative exercises, generally 5–15 minutes each. Kialo Edu cites Butchart et al. (2009) and Davies, Barnett & van Gelder (2019) for argument-mapping effectiveness.
- **MindMup** offers a free argument-visualization template and is used by university libraries (Saskatchewan, eCampusOntario) for short reconstruction exercises but has no dedicated effectiveness research.

A more rigorous Iranian study (Marandi et al., reported on ResearchGate) compared Rationale-based instruction to pencil-and-paper argument mapping and to traditional instruction; students using Rationale showed the highest post-test critical-thinking scores. A 2025 ScienceDirect paper (Zhejiang Provincial study) showed that systematically designed CSAV (computer-supported argument visualization) tutorials improve L2 students' argument-analysis and writing skills.

### "Critical Thinking in Global Challenges" (Edinburgh / Coursera)
A 5-week MOOC by Mayank Dutia and Celine Caquineau using global health topics. Exercises ask learners, given a paragraph about an epidemic policy or climate claim, to (a) classify each sentence as fact, opinion, or argument and (b) identify the implicit premise. Reviewers describe the quizzes as basic; no published RCT exists.

## 3. Distinguishing Fact from Opinion / Fact from Inference

### Checkology (News Literacy Project)
Checkology is a free e-learning platform for grades 5–12. While most "lessons" are full multi-media units, embedded **"Exercises"** are the genuinely micro-format components.

**Format.**
- **InfoZones exercises**: a real-world social media post, article, or video is shown; the student must classify it into one of six categories — news, opinion, propaganda, entertainment, raw information, or advertising — usually within a single screen and a few seconds of decision time. This is one of the cleanest "fact-vs-opinion-plus-others" micro-tasks in widespread classroom use.
- **"Two Facts and a Feeling"** (a grades 4–6 lesson): students see three short statements and identify which two are fact-based and which one is "a feeling" (opinion). They then write their own set of two facts and one feeling and apply the same labels to a news article.
- **"Is It Checkable?"**: students see a claim and decide whether it is the kind of statement that could in principle be verified (a factual claim) versus an opinion that cannot.
- **Check Center Missions**: longer fact-checking activities that combine several skills, but with multiple inner micro-decisions.

**Effectiveness.** A peer-reviewed evaluation by Vraga, Tully & Bode and a Northwestern/Harvard Kennedy School-cited internal evaluation report attitude change among Checkology students and improved ability to label claim types. Checkology says it is used by 18,000+ educators in all 50 US states and 100+ countries; rigorous external RCTs are limited.

### Factitious (American University Game Lab, 2017–18)
**Format.** Tinder-style swipe interface. The player sees a single news article (headline, lede, source hidden by default) and **swipes right if real, left if fake**. After each swipe, immediate feedback shows the source and a one-sentence rationale; players can spend points on a hint to reveal the source before swiping. Each item is ~10–30 seconds; the game's full round is 15 articles. Played 339,000+ times in the first month after launch and millions of times overall, it is a near-perfect example of a micro-exercise.

**Skill trained.** Distinguishing real reporting from satire/fake-news domains; secondarily, weighting source credibility.

**Effectiveness.** Co-designer Lindsay Grace's report (Factitious 1.0/2.0 paper) shows the average finishing player scores 82% correct, and that players' accuracy improves across rounds. There is no controlled trial.

### Fakey (Indiana University Observatory on Social Media)
Built by Mihai Avram, Filippo Menczer, and colleagues (Micallef et al., *ACM Digital Library* 2021).

**Format.** A simulated mobile news feed shows ten cards per round, each a real headline either from a "credible" source (Reuters, *Washington Post*, BuzzFeed news desk, etc.) or a low-credibility outlet (clickbait, junk-science, conspiracy). For each card, the player chooses one of four actions: **Like, Share, Skip, or Fact-check**. The "correct" play is to share/like reputable items and fact-check or skip dubious ones. A hint reveals the source at a small point cost. Each card is a 5–15-second decision.

**Skill trained.** Source-based fact-vs.-rumor sorting and modeling of healthy social-media-use heuristics. Note that "credibility" is here a heuristic shortcut — the game uses source provenance as a proxy rather than asking the player to evaluate the actual claims, a limitation reviewers (Columbia Teachers College's EdLab) have flagged.

**Effectiveness.** The 2021 paper analyzed 19 months of in-the-wild interactions and reports that user accuracy improves over multiple plays, but it is observational rather than a controlled trial.

### NewsFeed Defenders (iCivics + Annenberg Public Policy Center / FactCheck.org, 2018)
A simulation-style game (free on web and tablet apps) for grades 6–12.

**Format.** The player runs the fictional social media site "Newsably." Posts queue up; for each one, the player rapidly evaluates whether the post commits one of three problems — **hidden ads, viral deception (fake news), or false reporting** — or is a legitimate news post belonging on a topic-aligned feed. Players also rate posts on four journalistic dimensions: accuracy, transparency, trustworthiness, and impartiality. Levels progress from guest user to site admin over a "14-day" simulated period. Individual judgments are micro-tasks (about 30 seconds each).

**Effectiveness.** No published RCT. iCivics reaches roughly 200,000 educators and 5+ million students in the US.

### SIFT Method (Mike Caulfield)
Not a single tool but a procedural micro-protocol now embedded in dozens of university library tutorials and in Caulfield's free 5-part "Check, Please!" course (each lesson ~30 minutes).

**Format.** When encountering a claim or article, the user runs four short "moves":
- **S**top — pause and check emotional reaction.
- **I**nvestigate the source — open a new tab and Google or Wikipedia the publisher (a technique Caulfield calls "lateral reading" or "Just add Wikipedia").
- **F**ind better coverage — search for what other reputable outlets say about the claim.
- **T**race claims, quotes, and media to original context — find the upstream source.

The micro-exercise version many college librarians use: present a single suspect tweet, headline, or image, set a 90-second timer, and have the student do one or two SIFT moves and submit a short verdict.

**Effectiveness.** Wineburg & McGrew (Stanford History Education Group) showed that lateral reading dramatically outperforms vertical "checklist" methods like CRAAP; their 2017 study found 96% of high school students failed to challenge source credibility in a vertical task — a finding NLP cites in framing Checkology. SIFT has been formally compared to CRAAP in studies by Lowe et al. (2021) and Liu (2021), with SIFT generally producing more accurate source-judgment decisions in shorter time.

### Field-Goal Discrimination Tasks in Inoculation Studies
The most common dependent measure across the Cambridge inoculation studies is itself a micro-exercise format that is sometimes used as a standalone classroom drill: an **18-item Twitter-style headline rating task**. Each item is a single fictional or real social-media post; the participant rates it on a 7-point scale for manipulativeness, reliability, or willingness-to-share. Items are roughly half manipulative (containing one of the DEPICT techniques) and half non-manipulative. The task takes 5–10 minutes total and gives precise per-technique scores.

## 4. Mixed and Other Notable Tools

- **CoronaChampion** (UNDP Accelerator Lab India, 2020): a Tinder-style swipe game where players see a COVID claim (e.g., "rinsing your nose with saline prevents COVID-19") and swipe right for true, left for false. Pure debunking rather than prebunking.
- **iReporter** (BBC, 2018): a choose-your-own-adventure micro-game where the player is a BBC journalist deciding which social-media posts, political claims, and photos to trust. Targeted at ages 11–18. Decisions take ~20–60 seconds each. No published effectiveness research.
- **PolitiTruth**: a Tinder-style mobile app built on PolitiFact's fact-checked claims. Each round shows a single political claim and the player swipes True/Half-True/False. Strictly a fact-vs-falsehood discrimination task with no fallacy training.
- **Fake News: The Game** (ISL, Washington DC): an arcade-cabinet-style installation and app with the same swipe mechanic, deployed in DC bars; no formal evaluation.
- **Libertas Veritas** (Deakin University): an interactive narrative with 15 endings exploring choices about misinformation; longer-form than micro.
- **Crash Course Philosophy** (YouTube, Hank Green): primarily a video series; the only micro-exercise component is the in-video quiz pop-ups (single multiple-choice items appearing during fallacy or argument episodes). No formal effectiveness research; widely used as supplementary material.
- **Lumosity**: despite being on the user's list, Lumosity's games target perceptual speed, working memory, attention, and basic problem-solving rather than argumentation, fallacy detection, or fact-vs-opinion. The 2016 FTC settlement (US$2 million) explicitly faulted Lumosity for unsupported transfer claims, and there is no evidence Lumosity tasks improve critical thinking in the sense at issue here.

## 5. The Big Picture: What the Effectiveness Research Says

Two meta-analyses by Abrami and colleagues are the standard benchmarks for "does any of this actually work":

- **Abrami et al. (2008, *Review of Educational Research*)** synthesized 117 studies and 161 effect sizes (n=20,698) on critical-thinking instruction and found a weighted-average effect size **g+ = 0.341** (SD = 0.61) — modest but real.
- **Abrami et al. (2015, *RER*)**, the second-stage analysis with 341 effect sizes, found g+ = 0.30 (p < 0.001), again moderate. The two interventions that mattered most were (a) **opportunities for dialogue and discussion**, especially teacher-led, and (b) **exposure to authentic, situated problems** (such as the simulated newsfeeds in NewsFeed Defenders or the choice scenarios in Bad News). Mentoring also added value.
- A 2025 systematic review in *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications* on training to improve critical thinking against mis/disinformation found that only a minority of interventions deliberately taught CT through "infusion" approaches; most embedded CT in broader media-literacy frameworks. The review concludes that active learning methods substantially outperform passive instruction — consistent with Abrami's findings and with the inoculation-game results.

For the inoculation games specifically, the published evidence base is roughly:
- Multiple **preregistered RCTs** for Bad News, Harmony Square, and Go Viral! show robust effects on perceived manipulativeness and confidence.
- Effects on **actual discrimination** of true vs. false items are smaller and culture-dependent (the 2025 Indian replication failed).
- Effects **decay** over weeks unless boosted with periodic re-exposure.
- The Jigsaw/YouTube field experiment is the largest and most ecologically valid, with a clear ~5–10% boost in technique recognition at scale.

For argument mapping (Rationale-style assumption-spotting), effect sizes are larger but come from a smaller and more concentrated research community.

For fallacy-naming MOOCs and Cranky Uncle's mobile gameplay, the evidence is mostly within-subject pre/post, so the absolute magnitude of skill transfer is harder to judge.

## 6. Quick Reference: Which Tasks Are Truly Micro and Game-Like?

**Truly micro (single self-contained item, seconds to ~5 minutes), game-like, with at least some published effectiveness research:**
- Bad News, Harmony Square, Go Viral! (choice-scenario items inside ~10–15-min games)
- Cranky Uncle (true/false, pick-the-fallacy, name-the-fallacy items)
- Factitious (Tinder-swipe headline items)
- Jigsaw 90-second prebunking videos + single-item BrandLift quiz
- 18-item DEPICT headline-rating task used as the dependent variable in Cambridge studies

**Truly micro and game-like, but with weaker or only anecdotal effectiveness data:**
- Fakey (like/share/skip/fact-check cards)
- NewsFeed Defenders (post-moderation decisions)
- iReporter, PolitiTruth, CoronaChampion, Fake News: The Game
- Checkology InfoZones and "Two Facts and a Feeling" exercises

**Quiz-like rather than game-like, micro in size, modest effectiveness data:**
- Coursera "Think Again" inter-segment quizzes
- Edinburgh "Critical Thinking in Global Challenges" quizzes
- LSAT/TARA/TSA-style critical reasoning items

**Closer to lessons or full curricula than micro-exercises:**
- Argument Ninja Academy
- Cranky Uncle's classroom write-a-denial-argument assignments
- Most argument-mapping coursework in Rationale or Kialo (although individual "reorder claims" or "mark claim" tasks within them are micro)
- Checkology's full lessons and Check Center Missions
- SIFT taught as a multi-week curriculum (versus individual 90-second SIFT drills)

The strongest pattern across the evidence is that effective critical-thinking micro-exercises combine three features: (1) **active production or choice** rather than passive reception, (2) **immediate feedback** about whether the player labeled the technique correctly, and (3) **abstraction across content** (so the player learns to spot the structural error rather than memorize specific claims). The Cambridge inoculation games, Cranky Uncle, and the Rationale argument-mapping exercises each instantiate this pattern in different ways, and each has at least preliminary evidence that the resulting skill transfers to novel material — though, with the Indian Bad News replication and the modest Abrami meta-analytic effect sizes in mind, the field's confidence in generalizable, durable transfer is moderate rather than emphatic.
